Data Sovereignty

Data sovereignty refers to the concept that digital information is subject to the laws and governance structures within the nation where the data is collected, processed, or stored. As AI continues to progress, it will become more important to make sure companies are abiding by regulations to make sure individual rights are protected.

A school system is a good example to illustrate data sovereignty. In most countries, people are elected to education boards to determine where schools are located, how they operate daily, the topics that can be taught, and the books that can be used. Each country has its own set of rules that the schools within their country must follow.

Just as books are the knowledge source that a school uses to educate students, data is the knowledge source that is used to train algorithms so AI can make better decisions. Similarly, just as the books and topics taught in schools are controlled by the education board, each country has its rules and regulations that govern how the data used by AI is collected within its borders. The collection of data must comply with that country’s legal and regulatory framework.

The importance of data sovereignty appears in the following ways:

  • Legal Compliance: Each country has its own AI-related regulations that regulate data privacy, usage, and security. Examples are GDPR (European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation) in the EU and CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act in the United States).

  • Privacy Protection: Data sovereignty is designed to protect individuals’ personal data. For example, a healthcare provider is required to keep your personal health records private. If these regulations were not in place, confidential health records could be accessible from a public space that would risk unauthorized access and use.

    Similarly, the European Union’s GDPR states that if a media platform wants to use AI to gather and analyze the behavior of users visiting, the platform must obtain permission from the users before collecting data and must allow those users the ability to remove their consent at any time.

  • Trust and Accountability: For AI to be adopted by everyone, there must be trust that their data is used responsibly and that there are regulations in place to hold organizations accountable for misuse.

  • Economic Impact: Allowing countries to control their data resources can stimulate economic growth. AI-driven data centers create job growth and technological advancements.

The legal implications of data sovereignty require organizations to carefully consider how and where they store, process, and transfer data to ensure compliance with the various laws and regulations.

Like each country having rules on how goods can be exported, each country also has regulations on how data can be moved internationally. Cloud companies located in the US must comply with GDPR while the CLOUD Act allows U.S. law enforcement to access data stored abroad. Companies that operate internationally must make sure they comply with multiple regulations for each of the countries they operate in. For example, a company may have to comply with both GDPR and the CLOUD Act.

There are also domestic implications. Just as some countries require their natural resources to be processed domestically for economic benefits, countries also have strict data localization laws. These laws require that data collected within a country’s borders must be stored and processed locally. For example, Russia’s Federal Law on Personal Data requires that all data about Russian citizens be stored on local servers.
